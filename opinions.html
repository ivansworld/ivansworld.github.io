Alright, so here are some opinions of mine. Have fun reading them, I guess...


This time I decided to not be lazy and check my spelling so yeah... that's a thing.


Braces: Semi-voluntary torture and agony in the form of over-priced metal glued to your teeth and then bound by wires.
Seriously, having them is absolutely awful. Just thinking of the wires getting tightened and my teeth shifting all around my mouth
makes my entire mouth numb.


(Ughhhhh) Fortnite: Since I know that somebody will probably ask for my opinion on Fortnite, I guess that I'll put it here.
To be perfectly honest, I've only ever played the game once, and the forty seconds before I got killed by somebody were extremely
boring. Also, i'm pretty happy with my decision to not let it waste precious hard drive space/waste any of my time.


Wireless Internet: I'd say that it's pretty useful being able to have internet access all throughout the house without
having to use any bulky cables. However, if you only use a stationary desktop computer and have a horrible internet connection like
me, then using an ethernet cable seems like a pretty viable option.


Nerd Culture: I really don't get how somewhere along the line, calling somebody a "nerd" turned from being an insult
to a desirable and trendy label. It seems that "nerd culture" and pop culture have intersected, causing things such being a fan
of science fiction, playing games such as Dungeons and Dragons, and the dreaded (by me, at least) abomination known as "geek chic"
to become "hip" and "trendy." The same people who picked on actual nerds fifteen years ago are now calling themselves "nerds" just
because they watch Doctor Who and decide wear fake thick-rimmed glasses frames. Remember, if somebody calls themself a nerd or geek, then
chances are that they're just following the trend. Watching Star Wars or liking superheroes doesn't make one a nerd. If you know
every single fact about an obscure sci-fi series, have been made fun of or bullied for being obsessed with a particular subject,
or blow all of your hard-earned savings on collecting something such as vintage calculators or every single piece of Star Trek
memorabilia, then maybe you are an actual nerd after all... maybe

Or not, if that's not your kind of thing.







  Ha ha, NERRRRRRRRD!







Laptop Trackpads: Pretty much all of them completely suck to use. The trackpad's always not sensitive enough or
too sensitive, and when you glide your fingers over them, it either feels like you're touching a sticky pane of glass or
fine-grit sandpaper. To be honest, I greatly prefer IBM's trackpoint, AKA the red nub in the middle of all thinkpad keyboards.





  Ah... much better!






State Standardized Testing: I see it as a semi-viable way of measuring how well the students of a given school
are performing compared to state standards, but I dislike how the attitude with them is that if you get a bad score, then
you must be an idiot. You can know everything about a given subject and still score poorly on the test. There are so many
variables that is is impossible to tell one's true intellect from the results of a test


Social Media: Data-mining and targeted advertisements forced onto your screen thinly disguised as a "trendy"
way to share crappy photos of yourself with your obnoxious friends. These companies make all the money that they're
seen flashing left and right from selling your data and personal information to other, shadier companies.


DRM: Just another way of software companies saying "Even though you bought our product, you don't actually
own it so SCREW YOU!" If you buy a good, physical or software, then you have full ownership of said item and you
decide what you do with it. Besides, every single poorly made DRM scheme ends up getting cracked within a few weeks and
the software that you wrote ends up getting pirated anyways. Tip: Loyal customers and business customers will always
pay for your software, and pirates will always pirate. Absurd DRM measures hurt honest consumers and slightly hinder
pirates. I'm not staying connected to the internet to play your game, nor am I installing an absurd amount of anti-piracy
bloatware onto my computer.


Smart Devices: A prime example of the idiotic over complication of devices for the sake of "modernization".
I paid good money to build my computer, so why should I fork over even more so I can have your shitty "smart"
thermostat or "internet connected"fan or speaker that also records all of my conversation and sells them to others.
Computers are meant for computing. Phones are meant for calling. Fans are meant for cooling you down. Enough is enough.


Electric Vehicles: Definitely the future, although as of now they're not worth buying compared to vehicles with
a traditional combustion engine due to their (usually) higher cost, long and inconvenient charging times, expensive as
hell batteries that are bound to fail way sooner than the motor, and the fact that every single electric car seems to have a
zillion screens and apps and LEDs that make it more similar to a computer on wheels than an actual car.


PC RGB Lighting: Can look pretty neat if done well. An example of well-done PC lighting would be a few RGB fans
and maybe another smaller component like the motherboard. Just don't make that much of your budget revolve around it.


In-App Purchases: A tell-tale sign of a low quality application or game. Instead of dealing with a barrage of ads and
having your wallet emptied so you can get more "gems," just spend the extra few bucks to buy software that doesn't have any of
that bull.

School: Can be an interesting and even fun place if you go to a good one which also teaches subjects that you're
interested in. Otherwise, it's a complete and utter living hell. If you don't want to do anything other than flip hamburger
patties until you're old and gray, just stop wasting your time, your peer's time, and your teacher's time.


Sports: Mostly a bunch of guys kicking/hitting/throwing/bouncing a ball around some sort of stadium. I've never
really gotten why people get so passionate while watching them but then again, I've never really cared about that sort of
thing anyways.


Cross-Country running: I'm on the cross-country team and as somebody who isn't very good at anything to do with
physical activity, I'd describe it as "Horrible, excruciating pain while you're doing it but rewarding as eff in the end."


Books: See my "decent enough" reading list


Politics: Mostly pretty boring, to be honest. It's kind of absurd seeing tons of people from all sides of the argument
getting so passionate over the exact same things time and time again. Can't we all just quit fighting over silly purposely
dramatized scenarios designed to piss everybody off and work together to solve problems? Nevermind, politicians are amazing
people who could never tell a lie!


The News/Media: Related to the topic above, it's all just more fake manufactured lies to make the unaware masses go
absolutely berzerk. Buzzfeed, CNN, Fox News, Infowars, it's all the same stuff, it's just paraphrased a thousand times over. If you
are in search of the actual truth, then try forming your own opinions from your own experiences for once.


Crowdfunding Sites: I mean, somebody must have thought that it was a good idea to try to sell people an unfinished product
that doesn't even have a launch date yet considering that many people still keep falling into the same crowdfunding scams over and over
again. Seriously, hasn't anybody noticed any of the major examples of "Crowd Scamming" like the Batteriser and the overhyped "Solar
Freakin Roadways?"


Patriotism: I don't really have much of a problem with loving your country, until it turns into loving your country so much
that you feel superior to everybody else (Aka Nationalism). Also, Fourth of July BBQs are pretty nice to attend 'cause free food.


Toilet Paper: I still have no clue why it's acceptable in this country today to wipe you behind with a thin piece of paper
instead of just using a bidet and maybe a few moist wipes? Plus, it's a massive waste of paper. If I got a nickle for the number of
times that I've seen a public restroom absolutely covered in toilet paper, then i'd have a very large number of said nickles.


Watches: I've been wearing my Casio databank for a few years and I have to say, it's the best watch that I've ever owned.
It has lots of useful features like a calculator, a phonebook, dual time, etc. while still managing NOT to be a connected "smart"
device. Not only that, but I seem to get lots of people who don't know what it asking me why it has a keypad and complementing me.
I also owned a Pebble brand smartwatch for around half a year until I got so sick of having to charge the thing every other night and
connecting it to my phone just so it could quit nagging me about having no BlueTooth connection. Good riddance.


Desk fans: A godsend for those who don't have a house AC unit, such as myself.


Calculators: Another godsend, this time one for those who can't do/don't feel like making a calculation. Plus, vintage
models look pretty neat and can be pretty fun to fiddle with.


Obsolete hardware: If you have a vintage piece of technology, offer to give it to somebody who might want it before you
get rid of it. Otherwise, nobody really wants your kicked-in Dell Windows Vista tower from 2008 that has a hard time running microsoft
paint because of all the bloatware.


Cryptocurrencies: Everybody thought that Bitcoin was unstoppable, and then it crashed. Real money is way less volatile, and
the stock market is a better tried-and-true method of investing money to later make more than you invested. Also, all the stupid crypto
miners bought up every last effing graphics card during the Bitcoin craze and caused the prices for GPUs to be doubled. $1,000 for a
$500 card? No thanks.
